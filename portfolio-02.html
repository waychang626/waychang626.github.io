The History and Analysis of the 2020 US Election

  • Introduction

This academic quarter oversees the most important U.S election of our life time. As a college student in California, I, amongst many of my classmates, anxiously waits for the election to end in the way that most polls predict. The democratic nominee Joe Biden and his running mate Kamala Harris have been taking a commanding double digit lead 2 weeks prior to the election day, and the Republican incumbent president Donald Trump and Mike Pence aim to convince the undecided voters that, alike the 2016 presidential election, the polls don’t reflect on the actual outcome.

Although the claim that polls don’t at all reflect the current state of the race is borderline unscientific, the burning reminder of the election of 2016 ponders some important questions: what significance do polls hold if they are, sometimes at least, inaccurate? If the inaccuracies are given, what do the magnitude of those inaccuracies entail?

In this project, I aim to take a deep dive into the meaning of polling and its ties to the statistical technique of random sampling. The concepts of polling errors in both national and state elections will be discussed in strive of how polls are made and how they differ from each other. Most importantly, with evidence from the history of polling and swing states, I want to discuss whether votes like mine, casted from a certain blue state, matters.
